Abstract

Nitrogen pollution is the primary pollution and threat to Long Island Sound. Nitrogen reduces the amount to oxygen in the water. Without oxygen animals like fish, lobsters, and crabs struggle to breathe. High amounts of nitrogen can lead to toxic algae blooms forming which can lead to fish dying and loss of coastal marshes (Save The Sound, n.d.). We used the Chesapeake Bay as reference system to show what types of ecological restoration goals we should be working towards on the Long Island Sound. There are goals and objectives that should followed to be successful with reaching our restoration goals. Then we should examine different alternatives to understand what kind of restoration process we should follow. Once we do that, we can select our preferred alternative. The preferred alternative for reducing nitrogen on the Long Island Sound is upgrading the wastewater treatment systems This will take at least 10 years to complete but we should see the results of upgrading the wastewater treatment plans within the next few years. To be successful, we should have monitoring and evaluating guidelines in place to show if our restoration process was successful. Then we examine the what the likely outcomes will be once the wastewater treatment upgrades are completed.

 

Plan Part 1: Site identification: Long Island Sound

 

The Long Island Sound is an estuary where salt water from the Atlantic Ocean meets freshwater. 90% of the freshwater that comes into Long Island Sound flows in from three main rivers in Connecticut the Housatonic, the Thames, and the Connecticut rivers. The Long Island Sound is ~1,300 square miles. It stretches from New York City to southern Westchester County, CT, and the northern shores of Long Island (SoundWaters, 2015). It is estimated that there are over 18 trillion gallons of water in the Long Island Sound. The coastline is 600 miles long and is 21 miles long at the widest point of the Long Island Sound. The water in the Long Sound can be between 60 to 350 feet deep (SoundWaters, 2015).

 The climate for Long Island Sound is a warm-summer humid continental climate. In August, the highest water temperature is at 73.6°F, and the lowest is in February, with an average water temperature of 39°F (watertemperature.net, n.d.). The GPS Coordinates for the Long Island Sound are 41.0748° N, 72.9461° W (Find Latitude and Longitude, n.d.).

Long Island is a terminal moraine, a long ridge of sediment and rock that was pushed south by advancing glaciers and left behind during the retreat. These events led to creation of a large freshwater lake known as Lake Connecticut (Long Island Sound Study, 2015). Today, this is where Long Island Sound is found. There was large-scale watershed erosion that was from massive amounts of sediments. Then sea level rose with the melting glaciers ocean water flowed around Long Island, which formed the basin for Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, 2015).

The Long Island Sound was formed over 400 million years ago with metamorphic bedrock during the last ice age (Varekamp & Thomas, 2005). There was a collision of large tectonic plates that dramatically changed the landscape of Long Island Sound. Glacial Lake Connecticut drained when a barrier at its eastern end failed. The Long Island Sound basin then dried out (Varekamp & Thomas, 2005). This event led to the creation of north-shore-oriented bedrock grain sculpted by multiple glaciations. Eventually, rivers were formed from the melting glaciers, which led to coarse sediments deposits that created the rocky northern shoreline of Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, 2015). In contrast, the south shore of Long Island Sound was created from course sediments deposited during glacial retreat.

Long Island Sound is a unique habitat, and it is an estuary which means this ecosystem is consistently changing with the mixture of salt water and fresh water. This means that this ecosystem is about to support a wide variety of plants and animals. The Long Island Sound has a variety of habitats, including salt marshes, tidal flats, sandy beaches, and the subtidal zone (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.).

 In the Long Island Sound, several species of vascular plants include eelgrass, bayberry, marsh elder, saltwater cordgrass, and sea lavender. These plant species are an essential part of an ecosystem because several birds and fish can feed, breed, and raise their young on the Long Island Sound zone (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.). One interesting aspect of the vascular plant species that grow on the Long Island Sound is that they are an essential part of the ecosystem, whether alive or dead. Species such as the fiddler crabs, finfish, and shellfish will use the vascular plants as a food source. Other vascular plant species such as beach pea, dune grasses, and dusty miller grow on the beaches of the Long Island Sound (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.).

The vascular plant species are such an important part of the Long Island Sound ecosystem that New York passed its Tidal Wetlands Act in 1973 to recognize its importance (Warren et al., 2015). This act was important because it meant the Long Island Sound was now protected from dredging and filling. A large portion of the original marshes was destroyed due to human activities. The New York Department of Conservation estimates that about 1,395 acres or 8 percent of the original tidal wetlands have been destroyed (Warren et al., 2015).

The animals that are found are an essential part of the Long Island Sound tidal wetland ecosystem. The Long Island Sound is long, narrow, and shallow. This means it’s able to support a large variety of different species. There are over 1,200 invertebrates, 170 species of fish, and dozens of marine bird species found on the Long Island Sound (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.). Another type of species that is essential to the Long Island Sound is worms. These marine worms are a necessary part of the ecosystem because they consume decaying matter like algae and bacteria.  Marine worms are an essential food source for larger animals such as fish and birds (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.).

Some of the bird species that are found in Long Island Sound include herons, egrets, osprey, rails, and the salt-marsh sharp-tailed sparrow (Ammodramus caudacutus). This sparrow is currently listed as endangered, and it is considered a species of national conservation concern by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (Warren et al., 2015). This is due to multiple factors, including habitat degradation, invasive species, sediment erosion, and climate change (U.S. Geological Survey, n.d.).

The Long Island Sound Study was initiated in 1985 between a partnership between the federal government and two states, Connecticut and New York. In 1987, the Long Island Sound was designated "Estuary of National Significance" under the National Estuary Program. The National Estuary is conducted under Section 320 of the Clean Water Act to protect nationally significant estuaries from pollution, development, and overuse (Suffolk County Government, n.d.). The Management Conference for the Long Island Sound Study was convened in March of 1988. The Management Conference oversaw the development of the Comprehensive Conservation and Management Plan for protecting and improving the health of Long Island Sound by making recommendations on how to enhance the quality of water, habitat protection and educating the public about the Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.).

The Long Island Sound Comprehensive Conservation and Management Plan was approved in September 1994. This is a collaborative management plan between researchers, NGOs, and individuals. This group is working together to protect and improve the health of Long Island Sound. Additionally, The New York State Clean Water/ Clean Air Bond Act has provided over $200,000,000 for the Long Island Sound Comprehensive Conservation and Management Plan to restore the Long Island Sound (Suffolk County Government, n.d.).

Human activities have been a significant part of the degradation of the Long Island Sound. The Long Island had a human population growth after World War Two. Unfortunately, the Long Island Sound was negatively impacted by increased population growth (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.). There was now more pollution, habitat destruction, and restriction of the Long Island Sound. The Long Island Sound was now a place to dispose of human and commercial waste and paving over land increased runoff. These actions eventually led to the degradation of Long Island Sound water quality (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.).

Today, there have been improvements made to the Long Island Sound. Since 1998 the Long Island Sound Study has completed more the 250 restoration projects through the Habitat and Restoration Stewardship Work Group (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.). These restoration projects have included restoring access for fish from an area that was block by dams and restoring tidal wetlands (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.).

The future of Long Island Sound Restoration looks promising.  In 2020 there was a major seven-fold increase in the budget for Long Island Sound Study Projects. This means that there is more funding for projects like restoring habitats in the Leetes Island Marsh in Guilford, Connecticut and improving water quality (Long Island Sound Study, 2021). Additionally, future projects will focus on climate change, restoring coastal habitats, and researching the conditions that cause algal blooms to occur (Long Island Sound Study, 2021).

However, today there is still reasons why restoration still needs to happen on the Long Island Sound for various reasons. Large areas of Long Island Sound have low-quality habits for fish and shellfish due to hypoxia (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.). There is still pollution and human development negatively impacting the Long Island Sound. Beaches on the Long Island Sound are periodically closed to pathogen contamination found in the water (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.).

Another major environmental issue on the Long Island Sound is the impacts on the local fisheries. For decades fisheries on the Long Island Sound have been struggling due to many different factors including climate change, habitat loss, pollution and overfishing (Save the Sound, n.d.). At one time there was millions of river herring menhaden and found on the Long Island Sound.  This is a major issue because Fish are an essential part of the Long Island Sound ecosystem because fishing is a large portion of the local economy. The fishing industries found on Long Island Sound are worth hundreds of millions of dollars annually (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.).

There are more than 120 finfish species in the Long Island Sound.  Twenty-one species of tropical finfish stay here seasonally. There are at least 50 native finfish species (SSO 102 Marine Environments, n.d.). However, there are fish species that are declining in the Long Island Sound.  Today, there are only a few thousand the that are migrating to the rivers on the Long Island Sound (Save the Sound, n.d.).

In 2015, two very rare animals that are found in Long Island Sound were added to Connecticut’s list of Endangered, Threaten and Special Concern Species. The two animals listed were the radiated shanny (Ulvaria subbifurcata) and the Atlantic sea snail (Liparis atlanticus) (Benson, 2015). Both animals are tiny, and they only grow up to six inches, which very few people have seen these animals. The main reason why these animals are endangered is to warming waters due to climate change (Benson, 2015).  Other concerns are that these animals will get caught in lobster traps, or kids will see them on the rocks and start trying to play with them.  These animals have always been rare, but now it's much harder to find them (Benson, 2015).

One of the most significant environmental issues negatively impacting the Long Island Sound is the pathogens entering Long Island Sound's waters due to inadequately treated human sewage and animal wastes. The main reason for this is due to older sewer systems. Eventually, all that waste ends up in Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.). The ecological restoration goals for the Long Island Sound are to improve the water quality and limit habitat degradation. We need to ensure the Long Island Sound restoration plan has long-term plans to reach these goals.

Plan Part 2: Stakeholders/goals/options/regs – Long Island Sound

Stakeholders

The Long Island Sound restoration is part of a study that is a federal-state partnership through the EPA. This federal-state partnership also includes state officials from Connecticut and New York. They provided oversight for the study (U.S. Government Accountability Office, n.d.). This program invests in regional and community-based projects focused on restoring the Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, 2021). The federal-state partnership is a critical component of the restoration progress of the Long Island Sound. They provide the funding for restoration projects and create plans and cost estimates for the Long Island Sound (U.S. Government Accountability Office, n.d.).

One example of a source of funding is the Long Island Sound Futures Fund. This program is a cooperative agreement between the National Fish and Wildlife Foundation and the EPA. The Long Island, Sound Futures Fund, invests in regional and community-based projects focused on restoring the Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, 2021). Since 2005, the program has invested $22.8 million in 450 projects. With a recipient match of $40.2 million, the program has generated $63 million for local conservation and management projects (Long Island Sound Study, 2021). These projects have helped reopen 105 river miles, restored 773 acres of critical fish and wildlife habitat and open space. Additionally, the funds have also been used to treat 200 million gallons of pollution and educate over 3 million people from local communities (Long Island Sound Study, 2021).

The Long Island Sound Study has committees and work groups that help to implement the Comprehensive Conservation and Management Plan for the Long Island Sound. This is a partnership of federal, state, interstate, local agencies, universities, environmental groups, industries, and the public (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-a). The committees that make up the Long Island Sound Study include the Policy Committee, the Executive Steering Committee, Management Committee, Citizens Advisory Committee, and the Science and Technical Advisory Committee (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-a). Together these committees and work groups represent the Long Island Sound Study Management Conference.

The Work Groups include the Climate Change and Sentinel Monitoring Work Group, the Environmental Justice Work Group, the Habitat Restoration and Stewardship Work Group, the Nitrogen Coordination Work Group, the Public Involvement, and Education Work Group, Sustainable and Resilient Communities Work Group, the Water Quality Monitoring Work Group and Watersheds and Embayments Work Group (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-a).

The Citizens Advisory Committee is a volunteer organization made up of 35 members, including the Whaling Museum and Education Center at Cold Spring Harbor, Citizens Campaign for the Environment, Friends of the Bay, and Earthplace/Harbor Watch (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-a). This group provides ongoing advice to federal, state, and local government partners of the Management Committee (Save The Sound, 2014). Membership to join this committee is open to individuals representing environmental organizations, businesses, industries, local governments, and other public and private organizations in Connecticut and New York who are interested in restoring the Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-a).

The Habitat Restoration and Stewardship Work Group. This group is responsible for promoting the conservation, restoration, management of Long Island Sound. There are various government organizations and NGOs that are members of this work group. Some of these members include the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service Coastal Program, Connecticut Department of Energy and Environmental Protection, New England Interstate Water Pollution Control Commission, New York State Department of Environmental Conservation, Connecticut Sea Grant, Seatuck Environmental Association and Save the Sound (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.).

Additionally, the Stakeholder Team is a subcommittee of the Connecticut-New York BiState Marine Spatial Planning Working Group. This working group is composed of government agencies, NGOs, trade organizations, and other stakeholders. These groups collaborate on marine spatial planning on the Long Island Sound (Consensus Building Institute, 2016). These stakeholders include the U.S. Navy, U.S. Coast Guard, The Nature Conservancy, Stony Brook University, NOAA Fisheries, Long Island Marine Trades Association, and the Coastal Conservation Association (Consensus Building Institute, 2016).

I believe there is a need for the public to get involved with the restoration of the Long Island Sound, especially if they are not familiar with local environmental issues. Some examples of these stakeholders could be local schools, tourist industries, and recreational users. I would get these stakeholders involved by doing presentations at local schools, outdoor events, and beaches. The more the public understands, the more likely they will want to be involved with the restoration efforts of the Long Island Sound.

Goals and Objectives

Goals

Objectives

Reduce the excessive growth of plant plankton and macroalgae.

Reduce nitrogen loads discharge into Long Island Sound to prevent harmful algae blooms from forming.

Improve harbor and bay navigability

Reduce land erosion that occurs along the waterways of Long Island Sound to sustain both recreational and commercial activities along the harbors of Long Island Sound.

Increase public knowledge and engagement in the protection and restoration of Long Island Sound

Continuing to do outreach events at schools and public events on the restoration efforts of the Long Island Sound.

Increase Piping Plover Population

Improve the Long Island Sound habitat to help increase the wild bird population that lives on the Long Island Sound.

Nitrogen is the leading cause of water quality deterioration in the Long Island Sound's surface and groundwater. Nitrogen comes from wastewater such as sewage treatment plants, cesspools, fertilizers, and stormwater runoff (Department of Environmental Conservation, n.d.). The discharge from the cesspools and septic systems then goes into the groundwater, ending up in the Long Island Sound.

Reducing large amounts of nitrogen loads in the Long Island Sound is essential. If no change occurs, the large amounts of nitrogen can stimulate excess plant plankton and macroalgae. This is known as a process called eutrophication (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-d). When the microorganisms eat plankton decay, the oxygen consumed from the bacteria and the bottom waters becomes hypoxic. This can lead to increased stress or even suffocation in slow-moving animals such as fish. The excess nitrogen can also degrade marine habitats like marshlands. (Department of Environmental Conservation, n.d.). Additionally, harmful algae blooms can also form from eutrophication which can be harmful to humans (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-d). Excess nitrogen also contaminates the groundwater, the sole source of Long Island's drinking water (Department of Environmental Conservation, n.d.).

The harbor and bay navigability maintenance is critical for both recreational activities and commercial activities that occur along Long Island Sound. Land erosion is happening along the waterways of the Long Island Sound, which means that's boats would have trouble accessing the channels. This can become a problem if boats have limited water access for both safety concerns and economic concerns harbors (U. S Army Corps of Engineers, n.d.). The Long Island Sound is part of the Federal Navigation Channels, which are maintained and surveyed by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. These waterways need to be clear for transportation systems essential for economic and national security interests (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-d).

Public outreach is an essential part of maintaining the Long Island Sound. Future generations must understand the importance of the Long Island Sound when doing outreach events; it is crucial to get both the public involved and local government officials who might not be aware of the environmental issues surrounding the Long Island Sound (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-f).

 

Alternatives

Reducing large amounts of nitrogen loads in the Long Island Sound

Alternative 1: Increase groundwater monitoring by developing modeling tools that can assist with the understanding of nitrogen loading (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-e).

Alternative 2: Wastewater and sewer improvements in Nassau and Suffolk counties by providing grants to residents and small businesses to replace their outdated cesspool systems with an updated system that reduces nitrogen. This is critical to improving water quality and public health (Nassau County Government, 2021).

Alternative 3: No Action Alternative – reducing large amounts of nitrogen loads from the Long Island Sound does not happen. If nothing is done, then there will be an increase in the loss of fish and other wildlife. Shellfish will become poisoned, which can be harmful to humans when they eat it, and there will be a loss of water quality in the Long Island Sound, which will negatively impact the entire ecosystem (Save the Sound, 2019).

Improve harbor and bay navigability on the Long Island Sound

 

Alternative 1: Shoaling the channels would help widen the waterways of the Long Island Sound. Bigger Boats would have an easier time navigating the Long Island Sound. (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-d).

Alternative 2: Dredging and disposal would be an additional method that would widen the waterways of the Long Island Sound. If dredging occurs, bigger ships such as tankers and bulk cargo would have an easier time navigating the Long Island Sound. This would need to be done every ten years (U. S Army Corps of Engineers, n.d.).

Alternative 3: No Action Alternative - If no action is taken to improve the navigability on the Long Island Sound. It would become increasingly difficult for boats to enter the Long Island Sound. The channels would have to be continually widened to allow boats to access both the waterways and harbors (U. S Army Corps of Engineers, n.d.). It would be a substantial economic loss if boats could not access the Long Island Sound, which would eventually impact the local economy.

Increase public knowledge and engagement in the protection and restoration of Long Island Sound

 

Alternative 1: Build community awareness and appreciation of the Long Island Sound. A great way to do this would be community clean-up events (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-g).

Alternative 2: Increase the public and local groups' involvement with Long Island Protection. Continue to do outreach events at public events and local schools.

Alternative 3: No alternative – the public won't be aware of Long Island Sound's environmental issues. This could have a future negative impact on Long Island Sound without any public awareness.

Alternative 1: Increase the breeding pair of piping plovers over the next five years. Slowing increases breeding pairs. Storms have decreased the bird's population, so restoring the piping plover’s habitat may be necessary (Long Island Sound Study, n.d.-f).

Reducing large amounts of nitrogen loads in the Long Island Sound

The Long Island Nitrogen Action Plan is a collaborative effort between the Department of Environmental Conservation, the Long Island Regional Planning Council (LIRPC), Suffolk and Nassau counties, and stakeholders. LINAP focuses on best-reducing groundwater and surface water through management and policies (Department of Environmental Conservation, n.d.-b).

The primary goals of LINAP are to assess nitrogen pollution found in Long Island waters, identify nitrogen sources in surface waters and groundwater, establish nitrogen endpoints, and develop a plan to reduce nitrogen pollution (Department of Environmental Conservation, n.d.-b).

Plan Part 3: Reference system / preferred alternative

The Chesapeake Bay because it is the largest estuary in the United States. The Chesapeake Bay watershed stretches from Cooperstown, New York, to Norfolk, Virginia. The Chesapeake Bay flows through eight states, Delaware, Maryland, New York, Pennsylvania, Virginia, West Virginia, and the entire District of Columbia (Annapolis & Us, n.d.).

The Chesapeake Bay is the nation’s largest estuary. It is home to more then 3,700 species of plants and animals. Some of these animals include muskrats, beavers, blue crabs, and ribbed mussels. Half of the Chesapeake Bay’s water volume comes from saltwater of the Atlantic Ocean. The other half come from freshwater rivers and streams that drain into the Chesapeake Bay from its 64,000-square-mile watershed (National Park Service, 2016). In addition, there are various types of ecosystems that are found on the Chesapeake Bay such as forests, rivers and streams, aquatic reefs & pilings, beaches and tidal flats, and wetlands (National Park Service, 2016).

The forests of the Chesapeake Bay are essential part of the ecosystem. The forest provides clean water. The forests are known as riparian buffers which means they can reduce the amount of nutrients that is running into the Chesapeake Bay. The mature trees have a deep root system that can hold the soil in place. This helps with stabilizing streambanks and reducing erosion (National Park Service, 2016). Additionally, the forest can capture 85% of the nitrogen that falls on them from the air. This keeps it from running into the Chesapeake Bay.

One unique type of ecosystem that is found on the Chesapeake Bay is the aquatic reefs. These reefs are complex and diverse communities that are made up of densely packed oysters. The reefs are the largest source of hard surface on the Bay’s bottom. Usually, the bottom of the Chesapeake Bay is covered by soft sediment. Additionally, there are sandy beaches and tidal flat lines that make up thousands of miles of the Chesapeake Bay shoreline (National Park Service, 2016).

Unfortunately, The Chesapeake Bay is going through a similar restoration process as the Long Island Sound is right now, so I thought the restoration process in the Chesapeake Bay could also benefit the Long Island Sound. The Chesapeake Bay has lost half of its shoreline and wetlands. In addition, the Chesapeake Bay has lost 98 percent of its oysters due to development (Through Restoration, n.d.). The Long Island Sound has been dramatically changed due to development. This is one of the main reasons why the restoration process began on the Long Island Sound.

Restoring the Chesapeake Bay means restoring streams, creeks, and rivers from Alleghany Highlands to the Eastern Shore. It also means improving drinking water and beaches. Another important reason to restore the Chesapeake Bay is the economy; more tourists are likely to visit the Chesapeake Bay if it looks nice and healthy (Chesapeake Bay Foundation, n.d.).

 For the Chesapeake Bay, early restoration efforts were started in some states, but things fell short. However, in 2010 restoration projects began picking up again thanks to the Chesapeake Clean Water Blueprint. In June 2014 the Chesapeake Bay Watershead Agreement was signed. his agreement focused on the core problems that are impacting the Chesapeake Bay such as nutrient pollution and sediment pollution. The Chesapeake Bay Watershead Agreement laid out 10 broad goals and 31 outcomes to be achieved by 2025 or earlier (Wheeler, 2021).

Another critical component of the restoration process of the Chesapeake Bay is working with local farmers. This is important because local farmers are using fertilizers that end up in the Chesapeake Bay water due to runoff. Using the Chesapeake Bay as a reference ecosystem, we can understand what restoration projects work or don't work in a large urban estuary environment.

In 2018 the Chesapeake Bay water quality was so bad due to nitrogen loading. It was rated 33 out of 100 on the health index. It was listed on Environmental Protection Agency's "dirty waters" list. The largest sources of pollution were from agriculture. The most significant way to reduce the nitrogen levels in the Chesapeake Bay is to improve water treatment centers and focus on educating farmers about the impacts of fertilizer runoff (Chesapeake Bay Foundation, 2010).

Recently, a review of the Chesapeake program showed that several of the outcomes will unlikely be met before 2025 without some significant changes. There are outcomes that show that there is not enough data to collect to show whether enough improvements have been made (Wheeler, 2021). We can use the Chesapeake Bay as reference system to show us what kind of restoration goals we should be used in restoring the Long Island Sound.

Preferred Alternative

After reviewing all the scenarios and alternatives the preferred alternative I decided to focus on is updating the wastewater and sewer improvements in Nassau and Suffolk. This system wide would be able to happen due to grants. The county and state officials have set up grants to offset half of the initial costs, loans would help with the other half. Some towns are being funded by town-approved taxes. However, these sources of money may be limited so additional funding may be needed (Geismar, 2018).

The reasons why upgrading the wastewater and sewer improvements are so badly needed is due to the Southwest Sewer District project that was done in the 1970s. This project was supposed to the first step in establishing the county’s sewer infrastructure, but the Southwest Sewer District project was overwhelmed with costs, mismanagement, and corruption so the updates were not completed. All purposed sewer projects were politically toxic until 10 years ago (Geismar, 2018).

In September 2020, the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation had approved the Suffolk Country Subwatersheds Wastewater Plan (SWP) as the Nine Elements Watershed Plan. (Reclaim Our Water, 2020). This project was developed in partnership with the Long Island Nitrogen Action Plan. The SWP involves numerous stakeholders, project partners and technical experts (Suffolk Country Government, n.d.). This project was developed after years of intense research of evaluating all of Suffolk County’s water resources. The SWP provides a specific roadmap on how to address the nitrogen crisis through wastewater upgrades and other strategies that are focused on reducing nitrogen pollution (Suffolk Country Government, n.d.). The goal is to transition away from a traditional cesspool system over a 10-year period.

The costs of this project will vary, Suffolk County Officials have said the average costs of installing an upgraded wastewater treatment system will cost around $19, 200. However, engineers have said it can cost can be as high as $25,000 to $30,000 for some houses where soil composition is difficult to work with (Geismar, 2018).

There will also be additional fees from upfront costs and maintenance The first three years of maintenance costs would be covered by the manufacturers from the installation price. After those residents would be required to have an annual contract that will cost between $250-$300 a year according to Suffolk Country Officials (Geismar, 2018). However, these contracts cost could be much higher. The average costs for maintenance contract can range between $500 to $2,000 dollars a year depending on what needs to be replaced or repaired. Additionally, these upgrades also have electrical components that add to utility bills. The average cost would be $11 more month (Reclaim Our Water, 2020).  A rate of $0.10 per kilowatt-hour would be used to estimate electrical costs (Long Island Sound Study, 2015).

 Finally, once the systems are installed, they will have to be tested every three years for water quality which will cost around $200 dollars according to the installers. However, Suffolk Country Officials said the costs would be closer to $100. This cost would be included in the price of the operations and maintenance agreement (Geismar, 2018).

Expected Outcomes

The expected outcomes for the upgrading the wastewater treatment system in Suffolk County looks promising. This project would be a long-term project which would take place over the next ten years. The SWP would be completed in phases. The first phase would be a ramp-up of the program (Reclaim Our Water, 2020). Top priority would be establishing a stable and recurring revenue. Additionally, other top priorities would be prohibiting the use of wastewater disposal treatments that don’t remove nitrogen and continue to build the industry and implement voluntary programs such as Septic Improvement Program (Reclaim Our Water, 2020). The goal is to have phase one completed by 2023.

 In Phase II areas that required upgrades would be the first to have their wastewater treatment upgraded. These areas included areas that have are most vulnerable to waterbodies and areas where 0-2 years of groundwater are contributing to surface waters. (Reclaim Our Water, 2020). This is the most cost-effective approach to have. The goal is within two years of these water treatment updates there will be decrease of degradation of surface waters. It will take at least 10 years see the see the full results of the upgrades. (Reclaim Our Water, 2020).

 

Monitoring and Evaluation Plan

The Hempstead Bay Water Quality Program will provide the framework for future water quality monitoring programs. This water quality monitoring program is managed by the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation and the Long Island Regional Counsel. This plan plays an important role in the Long Island Nitrogen Plan (Long Island Regional Planning Council, n.d.). It assesses the level of nitrogen pollution that is Long Island’s surface and groundwater, identifies the sources of nitrogen pollution, determines nitrogen reduction endpoints, and takes actions that will reduce the nitrogen loads to groundwater and surface waters (Long Island Regional Planning Council, n.d.).

The water quality data that is collected from strategic locations in Hampton Bay will provided a baseline on nutrient loading. The results of this data will be used when upgrades to wastewater treatment is needed (Long Island Regional Planning Council, n.d.). This monitoring program will also look to understand atmospheric nitrogen deposition which is associated with emissions from fertilizers, transportation emissions and energy production. This information will be used to understand point sources and non-point source loading (Long Island Regional Planning Council, n.d.). This monitoring program will be a long-term program to understand how nitrogen pollution impacts the Long Island Sound. It is recommended that this water quality program is completed for the next 5 to 10 years to make sure the wastewater treatment upgrades are successful.

The evaluation plan for will consist of having pilot areas to test to see if wastewater management strategies are working. This is especially important in smaller towns and hamlets. These smaller towns are a challenge because there in minimal land available for wastewater upgrades. The preferred treatments alternatives for the pilot areas were developed in conjunction with the Suffolk County Department of Public Works and Suffolk County Department of Health Services (County & Bellone, 2019).

Outreach

Incorporating citizen science can become a key part of helping reduce nitrogen pollution in the Long Island Sound. Volunteers could help with collecting water sample data. Volunteer opportunities would be in collaboration with the Long Island Nitrogen Action Plan. These type of volunteer opportunities are beneficial because the public would become more knowledgeable on water quality issues that impact the Long Island Sound (New York State Department of Environmental Conservation & Long Island Regional Planning Council, 2016).

Additionally, more public outreach is needed on Septic Improvement Program. This program benefits home homeowners who decide to replace their cesspool or septic system. Homeowners who upgrade their septic system with the new technologies will be eligible for a grant of up to $30,000 from Suffolk County and New York State to offset the cost of one of the new systems. In addition to the grant, homeowners can qualify to finance the remaining cost of the systems over 15 years at a low 3% fixed interest rate (Reclaim our Water, n.d.).
